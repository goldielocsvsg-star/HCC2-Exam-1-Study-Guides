Understanding Stress & Coping

A biopsychosocial process that determines health outcomes

üß†

What IS Stress & Coping?

NOT an event. NOT a symptom. NOT a diagnosis.

It's a biopsychosocial PROCESS involving:

  • Biological: Neuroendocrine + immune responses
  • Psychological: Appraisal, perception, emotion
  • Social: Family, culture, environment, resources

Why Does It Matter?

This process determines whether a stressor becomes:

  • ‚úÖ Adaptive growth (resilience)
  • ‚ùå Chronic disease (breakdown)

The difference? Perception + Coping + Resources

‚è±Ô∏è Types of Stress (Acute, Episodic, Chronic)

+

Acute Stress

Short-term, reversible, adaptive. Most common type. Resolves when threat passes.

Episodic Acute

Repeated surges of acute stress. Common in perfectionists, high achievers, chronic worriers.

Chronic Stress

Constant activation of stress systems. This is where disease is born.

‚ö†Ô∏è Clinical Alert

Chronic stress is particularly harmful because it wears on individuals continuously, leading to adverse physical and psychological consequences across the lifespan.

üí° Remember!

This system is brilliant in emergencies and dangerous when prolonged. The same response that saves your life in acute danger destroys your health when chronic.

üéØ

Primary Appraisal

The first question your brain asks:

  • Is this harmful?
  • Is it threatening?
  • Is it meaningful to me?

Evaluates potential harm to self, loved ones, self-esteem, or values.

üîß

Secondary Appraisal

The next question:

  • What resources do I have?
  • Can I control this?
  • What are my options?

Determines if the problem is manageable within current capacity.

‚ö†Ô∏è Why Appraisal Matters

Incorrect appraisal = Incorrect stress response = Disease risk

The stress response only activates AFTER meaning is assigned. Two people facing identical situations can have completely different physiological responses based on their appraisal.

üè• Chronic Stress: Body System Breakdown (HIGH-YIELD!)

+

üß† Brain & CNS

Cortisol damages hippocampus ‚Üí memory loss, depression, anxiety, insomnia

‚ù§Ô∏è Cardiovascular

Persistent SNS activity ‚Üí hypertension, MI, stroke, arrhythmias

üõ°Ô∏è Immune

Cortisol suppresses WBCs ‚Üí infections, autoimmune disorders, cancer risk

üçΩÔ∏è Gastrointestinal

Reduced digestion + inflammation ‚Üí IBS, ulcers, gastritis, obesity

üí™ Musculoskeletal

Constant tension ‚Üí tension headaches, back pain, neck/shoulder pain

üß¨ Reproductive

Hormonal disruption ‚Üí infertility, ED, menstrual disorders, low libido

üìã Clinical Exemplars: Stress-Related Disorders

+

Anxiety Disorders

Maladaptive response to stress; ranges from mild to panic

Tension Headache

Most common headache; bilateral, dull, bandlike pain

Hypertension

Chronic elevated BP from sustained catecholamine release

Insomnia

Inability to fall/stay asleep; leading cause is stress

IBS

Functional syndrome with cramping, bloating, diarrhea; stress is trigger

Eczema

Chronic skin inflammation exacerbated by stress

Coping Strategies

Positive vs Maladaptive Responses

üîß

Problem-Focused Coping

Changes the SITUATION

Generating solutions to reduce or eliminate stressors. Taking action to manage or change circumstances.

Example: Scheduling a meeting to get more resources for an overwhelming project.

üí≠

Emotion-Focused Coping

Changes EMOTIONAL response

Regulating emotions (anger, fear, anxiety) through self-regulating thinking and behavior.

Example: Going for a run, talking to a friend, journaling about feelings.

üéØ

Meaning-Focused Coping

Changes INTERPRETATION

Drawing on values, beliefs, and goals to modify personal interpretation and response.

Example: Talking with a therapist to reframe and understand one's responses.

üí° Key Concept

The most effective coping is flexible and matched to the stressor. Problem-focused coping tends to be more effective overall, but the best approach depends on the situation, duration, and controllability of the stressor.

‚úÖ Positive Coping Strategies

  • Exercise/physical activity
  • Social support
  • Relaxation techniques
  • Meditation/mindfulness
  • Yoga/Tai Chi/Qigong
  • Music therapy
  • Art therapy
  • Prayer/spiritual resources
  • Counseling/talk therapy
  • Education about the stressor
  • Massage therapy
  • Guided imagery
  • Distraction/diversion

‚ùå Maladaptive Coping

  • Substance use/abuse
  • Avoidance coping
  • Denial
  • Social withdrawal/isolation
  • Excessive eating
  • Aggression/violence
  • Self-harm
  • Emotional outbursts
  • Displacement
  • Rationalization
  • Regression
  • Dissociation
  • Attacking/bullying

‚ö†Ô∏è Maladaptive = Short-term relief ‚Üí Long-term damage

Maladaptive coping responses ignore the underlying cause and often lead to additional problems. Avoidance can lead to increased emotional distress, longer recovery, and other maladaptive responses.

+

  • Muscle tension
  • Sweating/pupil dilation
  • Cognitive functioning
  • Emotional state/affect
  • Appearance & grooming

Common stress symptoms to assess: irritability, nervousness, feeling overwhelmed, depression, abdominal pain, headaches, insomnia, fatigue, lack of concentration, palpitations, chest tightness

üè• Prevention Model (Primary, Secondary, Tertiary)

+

Primary Prevention

Build resilience BEFORE illness
Nutrition, exercise, social support, healthy lifestyle, stress education

Secondary Prevention

Detect early
Screen for stress, depression, substance abuse. Identify maladaptive coping early.

Tertiary Care

Manage established illness
Treat stress-related conditions. Redirect to positive coping strategies.

üíä Pharmacologic Agents

+

No medications prevent or treat stress directly. However, medications may be used for stress-related conditions:

  • Anxiolytics (for anxiety)
  • Antidepressants (SSRIs, TCAs)
  • Hypnotics (for insomnia)
  • Muscle relaxants (for tension)
  • Antimigraine agents
  • Pain medications

Key Terms

Master these essential stress & coping vocabulary words

Stress

A series of reactions to external and internal demands; cumulative effect determined by duration, severity, resources, and coping actions.

Coping

Action directed toward change through conscious or unconscious thoughts and behaviors to avoid harm and restore balance.

Fight or Flight

Cannon's description of the immediate physiological response to perceived threat via SNS activation.

General Adaptation Syndrome

Selye's three-stage stress response: Alarm ‚Üí Resistance ‚Üí Exhaustion.

Transactional Theory

Lazarus & Folkman's model viewing stress as person-environment interaction with cognitive appraisal determining response.

HPA Axis

Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal axis; the neuroendocrine pathway activated during stress response.

Cortisol

Glucocorticoid released from adrenal cortex; mobilizes glucose, suppresses immune function when elevated chronically.

Catecholamines

Epinephrine, norepinephrine, dopamine; released by SNS to trigger fight-or-flight physiological changes.

CRF

Corticotropin-Releasing Factor; released by hypothalamus to initiate the stress response cascade.

ACTH

Adrenocorticotropic Hormone; released by pituitary to trigger cortisol release from adrenal cortex.

Primary Appraisal

Initial evaluation of potential harm to self, loved ones, self-esteem, or personal values.

Secondary Appraisal

Evaluation of resources available to overcome, reduce, or eliminate the stressor; assesses controllability.

Acute Stress

Short-term, reversible stress that resolves when the perceived threat passes.

Episodic Acute Stress

Repeated surges of acute stress; common in perfectionists and chronic worriers.

Chronic Stress

Constant, sustained stress that leads to adverse physical and psychological consequences.

Problem-Focused Coping

Generating solutions to change the situation causing stress; most effective type of coping.

Emotion-Focused Coping

Regulating emotional response to stress through self-regulating thoughts and behaviors.

Meaning-Focused Coping

Drawing on values and beliefs to modify personal interpretation of stressor.

Maladaptive Coping

Coping behaviors that don't address the underlying problem and may cause additional harm.

Homeostasis

State of physiological balance; the goal of successful stress management and coping.

Resilience

The ability to adapt positively to stress and adversity; recover from challenges.

ACEs

Adverse Childhood Experiences; linked with lasting negative effects on health into adulthood.
